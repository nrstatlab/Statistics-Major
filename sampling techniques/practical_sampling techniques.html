Practical Course — 7 Experiments

Semester III, Course 7 (Practical) — 2 hrs/week, Credit 1. Hands-on verifications of theoretical properties.

List of Practical Experiments (Official Syllabus)

  1. Show that the sample mean is an unbiased estimator of the population mean in SRSWOR and find its variance.
  2. Show that the sample mean square is an unbiased estimator of the population mean square in SRSWOR.
  3. Show that the sample mean is an unbiased estimator of the population mean in SRSWR and find its variance.
  4. Compare means and variances between SRSWR and SRSWOR.
  5. Allocate sample sizes among strata using proportional and optimum allocations.
  6. Compare the precision of proportional and optimum allocations with SRSWOR and compute the gain in efficiency.
  7. Systematic sampling with \(N = nk\) — compare its precision with Stratified and SRSWOR.

Experiment 1 — Unbiasedness of \(\bar y\) under SRSWOR

PROBLEM

Population: \(Y_1 = 2, Y_2 = 4, Y_3 = 6, Y_4 = 8\) (\(N = 4\)). \(\bar Y = 5\). Draw all SRSWOR of size \(n = 2\).

There are \(\binom{4}{2} = 6\) possible samples. Sample means:

Sample{Y₁,Y₂}{Y₁,Y₃}{Y₁,Y₄}{Y₂,Y₃}{Y₂,Y₄}{Y₃,Y₄}
\(\bar y\)345567

\(E(\bar y) = (3+4+5+5+6+7)/6 = 30/6 = 5 = \bar Y\) ✓ (unbiased).

Variance: \(E(\bar y - 5)^2 = (4+1+0+0+1+4)/6 = 10/6 = 1.667\).

Verify with formula: \(S^2 = \sum(Y_i - 5)^2/(N-1) = 20/3\). \(\text{Var}(\bar y) = (N-n)/(Nn) \cdot S^2 = (2/8)(20/3) = 40/24 = 1.667\) ✓.

Experiment 3 — Unbiasedness of \(\bar y\) under SRSWR

For the same population (\(Y_1=2, Y_2=4, Y_3=6, Y_4=8\)), draw all ordered samples of size 2 with replacement.

Total ordered samples = \(4^2 = 16\). By symmetry, mean of all sample means = \(\bar Y = 5\) ✓.

Variance: \(\text{Var}_{WR}(\bar y) = \sigma^2/n = (\sum(Y_i - 5)^2/N)/2 = (20/4)/2 = 5/2 = 2.5\).

Notice \(\text{Var}_{WR} = 2.5 > \text{Var}_{WOR} = 1.667\) — SRSWOR is more efficient.

Experiment 5 — Allocation of Sample Sizes (Proportional & Optimum)

PROBLEM

Three strata: \(N_1 = 200,\; N_2 = 300,\; N_3 = 500;\; S_1 = 4,\; S_2 = 6,\; S_3 = 8\). Total \(n = 50\).

Proportional allocation (\(n_h = n N_h / N\)):

  • \(n_1 = 50 \cdot 200/1000 = 10\)
  • \(n_2 = 50 \cdot 300/1000 = 15\)
  • \(n_3 = 50 \cdot 500/1000 = 25\)

Optimum (Neyman) allocation (\(n_h = n N_h S_h / \sum N_k S_k\)):

\(N_1 S_1 = 800,\; N_2 S_2 = 1800,\; N_3 S_3 = 4000\); sum = 6600.

  • \(n_1 = 50 \cdot 800/6600 = 6.06 \approx 6\)
  • \(n_2 = 50 \cdot 1800/6600 = 13.64 \approx 14\)
  • \(n_3 = 50 \cdot 4000/6600 = 30.30 \approx 30\)

Note that stratum 3 (large \(N\) and \(S\)) gets a much larger share under optimum allocation.

Experiment 6 — Precision Comparison & Gain in Efficiency

Using the same data: \(W_1 = 0.2,\; W_2 = 0.3,\; W_3 = 0.5;\; S_1^2 = 16, S_2^2 = 36, S_3^2 = 64\).

\(\sum W_h S_h^2 = 0.2(16) + 0.3(36) + 0.5(64) = 3.2 + 10.8 + 32 = 46\).

\(\sum W_h S_h = 0.2(4) + 0.3(6) + 0.5(8) = 0.8 + 1.8 + 4.0 = 6.6\).

Ignoring FPC (large \(N\)):

  • \(\text{Var}_{prop} = 46/50 = 0.920\)
  • \(\text{Var}_{opt} = 6.6^2/50 = 43.56/50 = 0.871\)
  • If \(S^2 \approx 50\) (population variance): \(\text{Var}_{SRSWOR} = 50/50 = 1.0\)

Order: \(\text{Var}_{opt} (0.871) < \text{Var}_{prop} (0.920) < \text{Var}_{SRS} (1.000)\).

Gain in efficiency over SRSWOR:

  • Proportional: 1.000/0.920 − 1 = 8.7 %
  • Optimum: 1.000/0.871 − 1 = 14.8 %

Experiment 7 — Systematic Sampling Comparison

PROBLEM

Population values for \(N = 16\) units: 2, 3, 5, 7, 9, 10, 12, 13, 15, 17, 18, 20, 22, 24, 25, 27. Take \(n = 4\), so \(k = 4\).

The four possible systematic samples (\(r = 1, 2, 3, 4\)):

rSample (units r, r+k, r+2k, r+3k)\(\bar y\)
1{2, 9, 15, 22}12.00
2{3, 10, 17, 24}13.50
3{5, 12, 18, 25}15.00
4{7, 13, 20, 27}16.75

Population mean \(\bar Y = 14.31\). \(E(\bar y_{sys}) = (12 + 13.5 + 15 + 16.75)/4 = 14.31\) ✓.

\(\text{Var}(\bar y_{sys}) = \dfrac{1}{4}\sum(\bar y_r - 14.31)^2 = [5.34 + 0.66 + 0.48 + 5.95]/4 = 3.11\).

Compare with SRSWOR: \(S^2 = \sum(Y_i - \bar Y)^2/(N-1) = 868.94/15 = 57.93\). \(\text{Var}_{SRSWOR} = (12/64)(57.93) = 10.86\).

Systematic is more efficient here (3.11 < 10.86) — typical for slowly-varying populations.

Stratified comparison (every group of \(k\) consecutive values forms a stratum of size \(k\), draw one unit per stratum): for this near-linear trend, stratified gives \(\text{Var} \approx (k^2 - 1)/(12 n) \cdot b^2\) which equals 1.25 — best of the three.
