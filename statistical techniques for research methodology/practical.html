Practical — Project Work

Develop a questionnaire, collect survey data pertaining to a research problem, interpret the results and draw inferences.

List of Experiments

NOTE

Each experiment requires the student to design a questionnaire, collect data from an appropriate sample, perform statistical analysis using software (R, SPSS, or MS Excel), interpret the results, and write a brief report following the format discussed in Unit 5.

Experiment 1: Gender Discrimination in Private vs. Government Sector

OBJECTIVE

To study whether there is a significant difference in the perception and experience of gender discrimination between employees in the private sector and the government sector.

Questionnaire Design

Develop a questionnaire with the following sections:

  • Screening: Current employment status, sector of employment.
  • Workplace Experience: 10-item Likert scale measuring perceived discrimination (e.g., "I have been passed over for promotion because of my gender"; "My salary is fair compared to colleagues of the opposite gender" — reverse coded). Rating: 1 (Strongly Disagree) to 5 (Strongly Agree).
  • Career Progression: Number of promotions, current designation level, years of experience.
  • Demographics: Age, gender, education, annual income range.

Data Collection

Stratified random sampling: 50 private sector + 50 government sector employees. Total: 100 respondents.

Statistical Analysis

  • Compute the total discrimination score for each respondent (sum of 10 Likert items, range 10–50).
  • Descriptive statistics: Mean, SD, median for each sector.
  • Two-sample t-test: \(H_0: \mu_{\text{private}} = \mu_{\text{govt}}\) vs. \(H_1: \mu_{\text{private}} \neq \mu_{\text{govt}}\).
  • Chi-square test: Association between sector and whether the respondent has personally experienced discrimination (Yes/No).
  • Logistic regression: Probability of experiencing discrimination as a function of sector, gender, and experience.

Expected Report Structure

Title, abstract, introduction (1 page), methodology (1 page), results with tables (2 pages), conclusions (0.5 page), questionnaire appendix.

Experiment 2: Unemployment Rates and Mental Health

OBJECTIVE

To investigate the relationship between unemployment duration and mental health outcomes, and to compare the psychological impact across demographic groups.

Questionnaire Design

  • Employment Status: Current status, duration of unemployment, reason for job loss.
  • Financial Impact: Percentage change in income, debt status.
  • Mental Health: Use the validated PHQ-9 depression screening scale (9 items, score 0–27).
  • Coping: Checklist of coping strategies, access to mental health support.
  • Demographics: Age, gender, education, previous occupation, location.

Data Collection

Purposive sampling of 100 unemployed individuals (50 recently unemployed <6 months, 50 long-term unemployed ≥6 months).

Statistical Analysis

  • Descriptive statistics: Mean PHQ-9 score by duration group.
  • Two-sample t-test: Compare PHQ-9 scores between recently and long-term unemployed.
  • Pearson correlation between unemployment duration (months) and PHQ-9 score.
  • Simple linear regression: PHQ-9 score = \(\beta_0 + \beta_1 \times \text{Duration}\).
  • Chi-square test: Association between severity category (minimal/mild/moderate/severe depression) and duration group.

Experiment 3: Impact of Subsidy Removal on Household Expenditure

OBJECTIVE

To assess the differential impact of subsidy removal on the service class and the unorganised sector workers, and to identify coping mechanisms adopted by affected households.

Questionnaire Design

  • Background: Employment sector (service/unorganised), household size, monthly income before and after subsidy removal.
  • Expenditure Change: Monthly cooking fuel expenditure before and after; total household expenditure change; percentage of income spent on fuel.
  • Coping Strategies: Checklist (reduced consumption, switched to cheaper fuel, took loan, reduced other expenses, no change).
  • Perceived Impact: 5-point Likert item: "The subsidy removal has severely affected my household budget."
  • Demographics: Age, gender, education, location.

Data Collection

Stratified sampling: 75 service class + 75 unorganised sector workers. Total: 150 respondents.

Statistical Analysis

  • Compute percentage increase in fuel expenditure: \(\frac{\text{After} - \text{Before}}{\text{Before}} \times 100\).
  • Descriptive statistics: Mean % increase by sector.
  • Two-sample t-test: Compare % increase between service class and unorganised sector.
  • Chi-square test: Association between sector and coping strategy.
  • Mann-Whitney U test: Compare perceived impact scores (ordinal data) between sectors.

Experiment 4: Online Shopping Behaviour Among College Students

OBJECTIVE

To study the factors influencing online shopping frequency and expenditure among college students and to examine gender differences in shopping preferences.

Questionnaire Design

  • Shopping Behaviour: Frequency of online purchases per month, average monthly online expenditure, preferred platforms, preferred payment method.
  • Factors: 8-item Likert scale rating the importance of price, convenience, variety, delivery speed, return policy, reviews, brand, and recommendations (1 = Not important to 5 = Very important).
  • Product Categories: Checklist of categories purchased online (electronics, clothing, food, books, etc.).
  • Demographics: Age, gender, course of study, monthly pocket money.

Data Collection

Convenience sampling of 100 college students (50 male, 50 female) from the campus.

Statistical Analysis

  • Descriptive statistics: Mean expenditure by gender, frequency distribution of product categories.
  • Two-sample t-test: Compare monthly online expenditure between male and female students.
  • Chi-square test: Association between gender and preferred product category.
  • Multiple regression: Monthly expenditure as a function of pocket money, frequency, and gender.
  • Factor analysis: Identify underlying dimensions of the 8 importance factors.

Experiment 5: Customer Satisfaction with Public Transport

OBJECTIVE

To measure customer satisfaction with public bus transport and identify the key determinants of overall satisfaction using regression analysis.

Questionnaire Design

  • Usage: Frequency of bus usage, typical route, time of travel.
  • Satisfaction Scale: 10 items on a 5-point Likert scale covering: punctuality, cleanliness, comfort, safety, fare affordability, route coverage, frequency, driver behaviour, seat availability, and overall satisfaction.
  • Suggestions: Open-ended question for improvement suggestions.
  • Demographics: Age, gender, occupation, income.

Data Collection

Intercept survey at 5 major bus stops. Systematic sampling: every 5th passenger. Target: 150 respondents.

Statistical Analysis

  • Compute Cronbach's alpha for the 10-item satisfaction scale.
  • Descriptive statistics: Mean satisfaction score for each item and overall.
  • Multiple regression: Overall satisfaction as dependent variable; 9 specific satisfaction items as predictors.
  • Identify which specific aspects (punctuality, cleanliness, etc.) are the strongest predictors of overall satisfaction.
  • ANOVA: Compare overall satisfaction across age groups or occupation categories.

Experiment 6: Awareness and Usage of Digital Payment Systems

OBJECTIVE

To study the awareness, adoption, and barriers to digital payment systems among different age groups and to identify factors predicting usage.

Questionnaire Design

  • Awareness: Checklist of digital payment methods known (UPI, cards, wallets, net banking, none).
  • Usage: Frequency of digital payments per week, average transaction amount, preferred method.
  • Barriers: 6-item Likert scale: security concerns, lack of smartphone, poor internet, preference for cash, merchant non-acceptance, complexity.
  • Demographics: Age, gender, education, income, urban/rural.

Data Collection

Quota sampling: 50 respondents each from age groups 18–25, 26–40, 41–60, 60+. Total: 200 respondents.

Statistical Analysis

  • Descriptive statistics: Awareness percentage for each method by age group.
  • Chi-square test: Association between age group and usage (user/non-user).
  • One-way ANOVA: Compare mean frequency of digital payments across age groups. Post-hoc Tukey test.
  • Binary logistic regression: Usage (Yes/No) as dependent variable; age, education, income, urban/rural as predictors.

Experiment 7: Study Habits and Academic Performance

OBJECTIVE

To examine the relationship between study habits (hours, method, environment) and academic performance, and to identify the strongest predictors of GPA.

Questionnaire Design

  • Study Habits: Daily study hours, study method (self-study / group / tutoring), study location (library / hostel / home), use of online resources (Yes/No).
  • Sleep and Health: Average sleep hours, exercise frequency, screen time.
  • Academic Performance: Current GPA, attendance percentage.
  • Demographics: Age, gender, year of study, stream.

Data Collection

Simple random sampling from the class register. Target: 80 students.

Statistical Analysis

  • Pearson correlation: Study hours vs. GPA, sleep hours vs. GPA.
  • Multiple regression: GPA = \(\beta_0 + \beta_1 \times \text{StudyHours} + \beta_2 \times \text{SleepHours} + \beta_3 \times \text{Attendance}\).
  • Two-sample t-test: GPA comparison between students who use online resources and those who don't.
  • One-way ANOVA: GPA by study method (self-study / group / tutoring).

Experiment 8: Food Consumption Patterns and Nutritional Awareness

OBJECTIVE

To study the food consumption patterns and nutritional awareness among urban and rural households, and to assess the relationship between nutritional knowledge and dietary quality.

Questionnaire Design

  • Dietary Pattern: Frequency of consuming fruits, vegetables, dairy, fast food, and sugary drinks per week.
  • Nutritional Knowledge: 10-item true/false quiz on basic nutrition facts (e.g., "Vitamin C helps absorb iron" — True).
  • Dietary Quality Score: Computed from consumption frequency (higher = healthier diet).
  • Demographics: Age, gender, education, income, urban/rural, household size.

Data Collection

Stratified sampling: 75 urban + 75 rural respondents. Total: 150.

Statistical Analysis

  • Descriptive statistics: Mean knowledge score and dietary quality score by location.
  • Two-sample t-test: Compare knowledge scores between urban and rural respondents.
  • Pearson correlation: Knowledge score vs. dietary quality score.
  • Simple regression: Dietary quality = \(\beta_0 + \beta_1 \times \text{KnowledgeScore}\).
  • Chi-square test: Association between education level and passing the nutrition quiz (score ≥ 7/10).

Experiment 9: Impact of Work-From-Home on Employee Productivity

OBJECTIVE

To study the impact of work-from-home (WFH) on self-rated productivity, work-life balance, and job satisfaction, and to compare outcomes across industries.

Questionnaire Design

  • WFH Status: Currently WFH (fully / hybrid / office), duration of WFH arrangement.
  • Productivity: Self-rated productivity change compared to office (5-point scale: much lower to much higher).
  • Work-Life Balance: 5-item Likert scale (e.g., "I can manage my personal responsibilities better while WFH").
  • Job Satisfaction: Single-item 5-point Likert: "Overall, I am satisfied with my current work arrangement."
  • Challenges: Checklist (internet issues, distractions, isolation, overwork, lack of equipment, none).
  • Demographics: Age, gender, industry, designation, years of experience.

Data Collection

Online survey distributed through LinkedIn and company email lists. Target: 120 respondents across at least 3 industries (IT, education, finance).

Statistical Analysis

  • Descriptive statistics: Distribution of productivity change ratings.
  • Chi-square test: Association between WFH status and productivity change.
  • One-way ANOVA: Compare mean work-life balance score across industries.
  • Ordinal logistic regression: Productivity change (ordinal) as dependent variable; WFH status, industry, experience as predictors.
  • Cronbach's alpha for the 5-item work-life balance scale.
