1. Formats of Reports

DEFINITION

A research report is a formal document that communicates the purpose, methodology, findings, and conclusions of a research study to a specified audience. It is the final and most critical output of any research project — a study that is not reported is, in effect, a study that was never done.

Research reports can take several formats depending on the audience and purpose:

1.1 Thesis / Dissertation

A comprehensive document submitted for an academic degree (Master's or Ph.D.). It must demonstrate the candidate's mastery of the subject, the originality of the contribution, and the rigour of the methodology. Typically 100–300 pages, it follows a strict format prescribed by the university.

1.2 Journal Article

A concise report (typically 3,000–8,000 words) submitted for publication in a peer-reviewed journal. It follows the IMRaD structure (Introduction, Methods, Results, and Discussion) and must conform to the journal's style guide (APA, MLA, Chicago, etc.).

1.3 Technical Report

A detailed report prepared for a sponsoring agency (government, corporation, or NGO). It emphasises methodology and findings with practical implications. Technical reports are not peer-reviewed but undergo internal quality checks. They may be 50–200 pages.

1.4 Executive Summary / Policy Brief

A very concise (1–5 pages) summary of key findings and recommendations for decision-makers who lack the time to read the full report. Focuses on actionable conclusions with minimal technical detail.

EXAMPLE 1 — Converting a Thesis into a Journal Article

A Ph.D. student has completed a 250-page dissertation on "Statistical Analysis of Factors Affecting Crop Yield in Telangana." To publish this as a journal article, the student must: (a) Reduce the literature review from 40 pages to 1–2 paragraphs. (b) Condense the methodology from 30 pages to 1 page. (c) Present only the most important results (3–4 key tables/figures instead of 25). (d) Focus the discussion on the novel contribution. (e) Compress the 250 pages into approximately 6,000 words (about 15 journal pages). The title is shortened to "Determinants of Paddy Yield in Telangana: A Multiple Regression Approach" — specific and informative.

EXAMPLE 2 — Writing an Executive Summary

A consulting firm completes a 120-page technical report on "Impact of GST on the Unorganised Sector in Andhra Pradesh" for the State Planning Commission. The executive summary (3 pages) states: (a) Background: GST was implemented in July 2017, replacing multiple indirect taxes. (b) Key Finding 1: 42% of unorganised sector businesses reported a decline in revenue of more than 20% in the first year. (c) Key Finding 2: Compliance costs increased by an average of ₹50,000 per year for small businesses. (d) Recommendation 1: Simplify the GST filing process for businesses with turnover below ₹20 lakh. (e) Recommendation 2: Provide GST compliance training through district industries centres. Each finding is presented in a single sentence with the supporting statistic, and each recommendation is actionable.

2. Parts of a Report

STANDARD STRUCTURE

A formal research report has a standardised structure that allows readers to locate specific information quickly. While formats vary by institution, the following components are universally expected in approximately this order:

#ComponentPurpose
1Cover and Title PageIdentify the report, author, and institution
2Introductory Pages (Preface, Acknowledgements)Set context and credit contributors
3Table of ContentsNavigate the report

7Chapter 2: Review of LiteratureSituate the study in existing knowledge

EXAMPLE 1 — Structure of a Survey-Based Research Report

A student writes a project report titled "A Statistical Study of Gender Discrimination in the Private vs. Government Sector." The report structure is:

  • Cover Page: Title, student name, roll number, department, university, supervisor name, month and year of submission.
  • Certificate: Signed by the supervisor certifying originality.
  • Declaration: Student's statement that the work is original.
  • Acknowledgements: Thanks to supervisor, respondents, and supporting institutions.
  • Abstract: 250-word summary covering objective, methodology (survey of 200 employees, t-test, chi-square), main finding (private sector employees report 21% more discrimination), and recommendation (policy reforms for private sector).
  • Chapter 1 — Introduction: Background, statement of the problem, objectives, hypotheses, scope and limitations.
  • Chapter 2 — Review of Literature: Summary of 8 previous studies on gender discrimination in Indian workplaces.
  • Chapter 3 — Research Methodology: Research design (cross-sectional survey), sampling method (stratified random, 100 private + 100 government), questionnaire description, data collection procedure, statistical tools used.
  • Chapter 4 — Data Analysis and Results: 12 tables with descriptive statistics, t-test results, chi-square results, logistic regression output. Each table followed by a paragraph of interpretation.
  • Chapter 5 — Conclusions and Recommendations: Summary of key findings, practical recommendations, directions for future research.
  • Bibliography: 25 references in APA format.
  • Appendix: Copy of the questionnaire.
EXAMPLE 2 — What Goes in Each Chapter

Consider a report on "Impact of Subsidy Removal on Household Expenditure." The Introduction chapter should answer: What is the problem? (Subsidy on LPG was removed, affecting household budgets.) Why does it matter? (Millions of poor households depend on subsidised fuel.) What are the specific objectives? (To estimate the increase in fuel expenditure, compare impact across income groups, identify coping strategies.) What are the hypotheses? (H₀: Mean expenditure increase is the same for all income groups; H₁: Lower income groups experienced a larger increase.) The Methodology chapter describes the sampling (300 households, stratified by income), the questionnaire, and the statistical tests (ANOVA, post-hoc comparisons). The Results chapter presents the ANOVA table showing F = 24.3, p < 0.001, and post-hoc tests showing that the lowest income group had a significantly larger percentage increase than all other groups. The Conclusions chapter states that subsidy removal disproportionately burdened the poorest households and recommends targeted cash transfers as a compensatory mechanism.

3. Cover and Title Page

KEY CONCEPT

The cover and title page are the first things a reader sees. They must convey the essential identifying information clearly and professionally. A well-designed cover creates a positive first impression and immediately communicates the scope and nature of the study.

3.1 Elements of the Title Page

  • Title of the research: Should be concise (10–15 words), specific, and informative. Avoid vague titles like "A Study of Consumer Behaviour." Instead: "A Statistical Analysis of Factors Influencing Online Shopping Behaviour Among Urban Consumers in Hyderabad."
  • Author's name: Full name as registered with the institution.
  • Registration / Roll number: As per university records.
  • Degree for which submitted: e.g., "Submitted in partial fulfilment of the requirements for the degree of Master of Science in Statistics."
  • Department and University: e.g., "Department of Statistics, Yogi Vemana University, Kadapa."
  • Supervisor's name: Name and designation of the research guide.
  • Month and Year of submission: e.g., "March 2025."
  • Logo: University emblem or logo, if prescribed.

3.2 Qualities of a Good Title

  • Specific: Clearly identifies the variables, population, and context.
  • Concise: Avoids unnecessary words ("A Study of," "An Investigation into").
  • Accurate: Faithfully reflects the content of the report.
  • Searchable: Contains keywords that researchers might use to find the study.
EXAMPLE 1 — Good vs. Poor Titles

Poor titles:

  • "A Study of Statistics" — Too vague; doesn't specify what aspect of statistics or what population.
  • "An Investigation into the Various Factors That Affect the Academic Performance of Students in Indian Universities and Colleges" — Too long and wordy.

Good titles:

  • "Determinants of Academic Performance: A Regression Analysis of Indian University Students" — Specific, identifies the method and population.
  • "Statistical Quality Control in Pharmaceutical Manufacturing: An Application of Shewhart Control Charts" — Precise, identifies the industry and method.
  • "Sample Size Determination for Two-Arm Clinical Trials with Time-to-Event Endpoints" — Highly specific, mentions the exact type of trial and outcome.

4. Introductory Pages

KEY CONCEPT

The introductory pages precede the main text and provide essential context, credits, and navigation aids. They include the certificate, declaration, acknowledgements, table of contents, list of tables, list of figures, and abstract. Each serves a distinct purpose.

4.1 Certificate

A formal statement by the research supervisor certifying that the report is a bona fide record of research carried out by the candidate under their guidance, that it has not been submitted elsewhere for any degree, and that it meets the requirements of the university. Signed and dated.

4.2 Declaration

A statement by the candidate declaring that the work is original, has not been plagiarised, and has not been submitted elsewhere. Signed and dated.

4.3 Acknowledgements

The author thanks individuals and institutions who contributed to the research. This typically includes: the supervisor, faculty members, survey respondents, funding agencies, family, and friends. The acknowledgements should be sincere and specific — not merely a generic list of names.

4.4 Table of Contents

Lists all chapter titles, section headings, and sub-section headings with their page numbers. Uses a consistent numbering system (e.g., Chapter 1, 1.1, 1.1.1). Should be generated automatically by word processing software to avoid errors when pages shift.

4.5 Abstract

A concise summary (150–300 words for a thesis, 100–250 for a journal article) covering: the research problem, methodology, main findings, and conclusions. It should stand alone — a reader should understand the essence of the study from the abstract without reading the full report. Keywords (5–7) are listed at the end for indexing and searchability.

EXAMPLE 1 — Writing an Effective Abstract

Abstract: This study investigates the factors affecting crop yield in the Rayalaseema region of Andhra Pradesh using multiple regression analysis. Data on yield, rainfall, fertiliser usage, soil quality, and irrigation access were collected from 120 farms through stratified random sampling. The fitted model (\(R^2 = 0.78\), F = 82.3, p < 0.001) shows that rainfall (\(\beta = 0.35\), p < 0.001), fertiliser (\(\beta = 0.22\), p = 0.003), and irrigation access (\(\beta = 0.18\), p = 0.012) are significant predictors of yield, while soil quality is not significant after controlling for other factors. The model predicts that a 1 cm increase in rainfall is associated with a 0.35 tonnes/ha increase in yield, holding other variables constant. Policy recommendations include investment in irrigation infrastructure and targeted fertiliser subsidies for rain-fed farms.

Keywords: Crop yield, multiple regression, Rayalaseema, agricultural economics, stratified sampling

5. Text of the Report

KEY CONCEPT

The main text is the heart of the report. It presents the research in a logical, coherent narrative that guides the reader from the research problem through the methodology, results, and conclusions. Each chapter should flow naturally into the next, building a cumulative argument.

5.1 Writing Style

  • Formal and objective: Use the third person or passive voice ("The data were collected" rather than "I collected the data"). Avoid emotional or subjective language.
  • Clear and concise: Every sentence should contribute to the argument. Avoid unnecessary jargon, repetition, and verbosity.
  • Precise: Use exact numbers and statistical values rather than vague qualifiers ("The mean increased from 42.3 to 56.7" rather than "The mean went up a lot").
  • Past tense: Use past tense for the methodology and results (what was done and what was found). Use present tense for established facts and general truths.
  • Consistent terminology: Use the same term for the same concept throughout. Do not alternate between "respondents" and "participants" and "subjects."

5.2 Use of Tables and Figures

  • Every table and figure must be numbered sequentially (Table 4.1, Figure 3.2) and given a descriptive title.
  • Refer to every table and figure in the text before it appears.
  • Do not simply repeat the table numbers in the text — interpret them. Instead of "Table 4.2 shows the means," write "As shown in Table 4.2, private sector employees reported significantly higher perceived discrimination (M = 32.5) than government employees (M = 26.8)."
  • Tables display exact numbers; figures display patterns and trends. Choose the format that best communicates the finding.
  • Follow a consistent format for tables (APA style is recommended): horizontal rules at top and bottom only, no vertical rules, clear column headers.

5.3 Reference and Citation

Every statement of fact, theory, or finding from another source must be cited. Common styles include APA (American Psychological Association), MLA (Modern Language Association), and IEEE. In statistics and social sciences, APA is most widely used.

APA IN-TEXT CITATION FORMAT
  • One author: (Kothari, 2009)
  • Two authors: (Cochran & Cox, 1957)
  • Three or more authors: (Montgomery et al., 2012)
  • Direct quote with page: (Kothari, 2009, p. 45)
  • Multiple sources: (Cochran, 1977; Kothari, 2009; Montgomery, 2012)
EXAMPLE 1 — Writing the Results Chapter

A well-written results paragraph:

"A two-sample t-test was conducted to compare the mean gender discrimination scores of private sector (M = 32.5, SD = 8.2) and government sector (M = 26.8, SD = 7.5) employees. Levene's test indicated equal variances (F = 2.14, p = 0.145), so the pooled variance t-test was used. The results indicated a significant difference, t(198) = 5.13, p < 0.001, with private sector employees scoring 5.7 points higher on average. The 95% confidence interval for the difference was [3.51, 7.89]. The effect size, Cohen's d = 0.725, indicates a medium-to-large practical significance (Cohen, 1988). These results support Hypothesis H₁ that private sector employees perceive more gender discrimination than government sector employees."

Note the structure: what was done → what was found → what it means → how it relates to the hypothesis. Every statistical result is accompanied by an effect size and confidence interval, not just a p-value.

EXAMPLE 2 — Common Writing Errors and Corrections

Error 1 — Vague language: "The results were significant." → Correction: "The difference in mean scores was statistically significant at the 0.05 level (t = 3.42, df = 98, p = 0.001)."

Error 2 — Missing context: "The correlation was 0.65." → Correction: "Pearson correlation between study hours and exam score was r = 0.65 (n = 80, p < 0.001), indicating a moderately strong positive association."

Error 3 — Confusing significance with importance: "The result was highly significant (p < 0.001), proving that the drug is effective." → Correction: "The difference was statistically significant (p < 0.001), but the mean reduction of 0.3 mm Hg (95% CI: [0.1, 0.5]) is clinically negligible, suggesting the drug has no meaningful therapeutic effect."

Error 4 — Unreferenced claims: "It is well known that women are paid less than men." → Correction: "Gender-based wage disparities have been documented across industries (ILO, 2022; Blau & Kahn, 2017), with women in India earning approximately 20% less than men for comparable work (Monetary Policy Committee Report, 2023)."

6. Reference Section

KEY CONCEPT

The reference section (bibliography) lists all sources cited in the report. It serves three purposes: (a) gives credit to original authors, preventing plagiarism; (b) allows readers to locate and verify the cited sources; (c) demonstrates the depth and breadth of the researcher's engagement with existing literature.

6.1 APA Reference Format (7th Edition)

COMMON REFERENCE FORMATS

Book: Author, A. A. (Year). Title of book (Edition). Publisher.

Example: Kothari, C. R. (2009). Research methodology: Methods and techniques (2nd rev. ed.). New Age International.

Journal Article: Author, A. A., & Author, B. B. (Year). Title of article. Journal Name, Volume(Issue), Page range. https://doi.org/xxxxx

Example: Montgomery, D. C., & Woodall, W. H. (2008). An overview of six sigma. International Statistical Review, 76(3), 329–346.

Website: Author, A. A. (Year, Month Day). Title of page. Site Name. URL

Example: World Health Organization. (2023, May 15). Clinical trials. https://www.who.int/clinical-trials

6.2 Important Rules

  • List references in alphabetical order by the first author's surname.
  • Every in-text citation must have a corresponding entry in the reference list, and vice versa.
  • Use hanging indent (first line flush left, subsequent lines indented 0.5 inch).
  • Include DOIs for journal articles whenever available.
  • Do not list sources you have not actually cited in the text.
EXAMPLE 1 — Reference List for a Statistics Project

A sample reference list:

  • Cochran, W. G. (1977). Sampling techniques (3rd ed.). John Wiley & Sons.
  • Cohen, J. (1988). Statistical power analysis for the behavioral sciences (2nd ed.). Lawrence Erlbaum Associates.
  • Kothari, C. R. (2009). Research methodology: Methods and techniques (2nd rev. ed.). New Age International.
  • Montgomery, D. C. (2013). Statistical quality control: A modern introduction (7th ed.). John Wiley & Sons.
  • Montgomery, D. C., & Woodall, W. H. (2008). An overview of six sigma. International Statistical Review, 76(3), 329–346. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1751-5823.2008.00048.x
EXAMPLE 2 — What Not to Do in the Reference Section

Common errors to avoid:

  • Padding: Including 50 references when only 15 were actually cited. This is dishonest and easily detected by comparing the in-text citations with the reference list.
  • Incomplete entries: "Kothari, C.R. — Research Methodology" without publisher, year, or edition. A reader cannot locate this source.
  • Inconsistent style: Mixing APA and MLA formats in the same list. Pick one style and use it consistently.
  • Outdated sources without justification: Citing a 1950 sampling textbook when a 2020 edition exists, unless the original formulation is specifically being discussed.
  • Missing DOIs: For journal articles available online, the DOI is required in APA 7th edition.

7. Typing Instructions

STANDARD FORMAT

Most universities prescribe specific typing and formatting instructions for project reports and theses. While these vary by institution, the following are widely accepted standards.

7.1 Page Setup

  • Paper size: A4 (210 × 297 mm).
  • Margins: Left margin 1.5 inches (for binding); top, bottom, and right margins 1 inch each.
  • Font: Times New Roman, 12 pt for body text; 14 pt bold for chapter titles; 12 pt bold for section headings.
  • Line spacing: 1.5 or double-spaced for body text; single-spaced for block quotations, table notes, and reference entries.
  • Paragraph indentation: First line of each paragraph indented 0.5 inch (or 1 tab stop).
  • Page numbers: Preliminary pages numbered in lowercase Roman numerals (i, ii, iii…); main text numbered in Arabic numerals (1, 2, 3…). Page numbers on the top right corner.

7.2 Headings

  • Chapter heading: Centre, 14 pt bold, all caps. Start on a new page.
  • Section heading: Left-aligned, 12 pt bold, title case.
  • Sub-section heading: Left-aligned, 12 pt bold italic, title case.

7.3 Tables and Figures

  • Table number and title above the table (e.g., "Table 4.2: Comparison of Mean Scores by Sector").
  • Figure number and title below the figure (e.g., "Figure 3.1: Scatter Plot of Yield vs. Rainfall").
  • Each table/figure on a separate page or embedded in the text with adequate margins.
  • Font size in tables: 10–11 pt. Minimum font for readability.

EXAMPLE 2 — Common Formatting Mistakes

Mistake 1: Using different fonts for different chapters. → Fix: Use the same font (Times New Roman 12 pt) throughout.

Mistake 2: Tables with vertical lines and excessive horizontal lines. → Fix: Use APA format — top border, header border, bottom border only.

Mistake 3: Starting a new chapter on the same page as the previous chapter's last paragraph. → Fix: Each chapter starts on a new page.

Mistake 4: Inconsistent page numbering — Arabic numbers on preliminary pages. → Fix: Roman numerals (i, ii, iii) for preliminary pages; Arabic (1, 2, 3) for main text.

8. Copy Reading and Proof Reading

KEY CONCEPT

Copy reading (copy editing) is the process of reviewing the report for clarity, consistency, grammar, and style before the final version is prepared. Proof reading is the final check of the typeset or printed version against the edited manuscript to catch typographical errors, formatting inconsistencies, and layout problems. Both are essential for a professional-quality report.

8.1 Copy Reading Checklist

  • Grammar and syntax: Subject-verb agreement, tense consistency, proper sentence structure.
  • Spelling: Check all words, especially technical terms, author names, and foreign words.
  • Punctuation: Consistent use of commas, semicolons, colons, and apostrophes.
  • Terminology: Consistent use of the same term for the same concept throughout.
  • Numbers: Consistent formatting of statistical values (2 decimal places for p-values, 3 for test statistics, etc.).
  • Citations: Every in-text citation matches a reference list entry and vice versa.
  • Cross-references: "Table 4.2" actually exists and is correctly numbered.
  • Logical flow: Paragraphs and sections follow a coherent argument.

8.2 Proof Reading Checklist

  • Page numbers: Sequential and correctly placed.
  • Headers and footers: Correct chapter title or running head on each page.
  • Table of contents: All page numbers match the actual page of each section.
  • Tables and figures: Correctly placed, not split across pages (unless necessary).
  • Widows and orphans: No single lines of a paragraph at the bottom of one page or the top of the next.
  • Blank pages: No unintended blank pages in the document.
  • Consistent formatting: Font sizes, heading styles, margins, and spacing are consistent throughout.
EXAMPLE 1 — Copy Reading Corrections

A student's draft contains the following errors that copy reading catches:

  • Grammar: "The data was collected from 200 respondent" → "The data were collected from 200 respondents" (data is plural; respondent needs plural s).
  • Inconsistency: Chapter 2 uses "questionnaire" but Chapter 3 uses "survey form" for the same instrument → Standardise to "questionnaire" throughout.
  • Number formatting: p = .03 in one place, p = 0.03 in another, p = .030 in a third → Standardise to p = .03 (APA format, no leading zero for p-values).
  • Missing citation: "According to Cochran (1977), the optimal allocation in stratified sampling is…" but Cochran (1977) is not in the reference list → Add the full reference.
  • Vague reference: "As shown in the table above" → "As shown in Table 4.2" (specific cross-reference).
EXAMPLE 2 — Proof Reading a Final Print

Before submitting the bound thesis, the student prints a copy and proof reads it. Issues found:

  • Page 23 has a figure that is split across two pages (bottom half on page 23, top half on page 24). → Move the figure to the top of page 24 with a page break before it.
  • The Table of Contents lists Chapter 4 on page 47, but it actually starts on page 49 because Chapter 3 ran longer than expected. → Update the Table of Contents.
  • Page 67 has a single line ("The results are discussed in the next section.") at the top of the page (orphan). → Adjust spacing to move it to the previous page or bring the next paragraph up.
  • The supervisor's name is misspelt on the title page ("Dr. S. Lakshmidevama" instead of "Dr. S. Lakshmi Devamma"). → Correct immediately — this is a serious error.
  • Reference number 12 has a broken URL (returns 404). → Verify the URL and update if the page has moved, or replace with a DOI if available.

9. Presentation of a Report

KEY CONCEPT

A research presentation is an oral (and visual) communication of the research findings to an audience — a committee, conference, or stakeholders. While the written report is comprehensive, the presentation must be concise, engaging, and focused on the key message. A good presentation complements the written report; it does not replace it.

9.1 Communication Dimensions

Effective presentations operate on three dimensions:

  • Content (what you say): The substance — key findings, methodology, conclusions. Must be accurate, relevant, and logically structured.
  • Organisation (how you structure it): The flow — a clear beginning (problem), middle (methods and results), and end (conclusions). Use signposting ("First, I will discuss…; Next, I will present…; Finally, I will conclude…").
  • Delivery (how you say it): Voice, body language, eye contact, and timing. A well-organised content poorly delivered is as ineffective as a poorly organised content well delivered.

9.2 Presentation Package

A typical academic presentation includes:

  • Title slide: Title, author, affiliation, date.
  • Introduction (1–2 slides): Research problem and objectives.
  • Literature review (1 slide): Brief summary of key prior work and the research gap.
  • Methodology (2–3 slides): Study design, sampling, data collection, and analytical methods.
  • Results (3–5 slides): Key tables and figures with interpretation. Do not show raw output — present clean, formatted results.
  • Discussion (1–2 slides): Interpretation of results in context.
  • Conclusions (1 slide): Summary of key findings and recommendations.
  • References (1 slide): Key references only.
  • Thank you / Questions slide.

9.3 Timing

A typical thesis defence or conference presentation is 15–20 minutes with 5–10 minutes for questions. Allocate approximately: Introduction 10%, Literature 5%, Methodology 15%, Results 40%, Discussion 20%, Conclusions 10%.

EXAMPLE 1 — Structuring a 15-Minute Thesis Presentation

A student presenting "Gender Discrimination in Private vs. Government Sector" allocates the 15 minutes as follows:

  • Minutes 1–2 (Introduction): "Gender discrimination in the workplace remains a significant concern in India. This study compares perceived discrimination in the private and government sectors. The objectives are…" (2 slides)
  • Minute 3 (Literature): "Previous studies (Gupta, 2018; Rao, 2020) have documented wage gaps, but none directly compared discrimination perception across sectors." (1 slide)
  • Minutes 4–6 (Methodology): Stratified random sampling, 100 private + 100 government employees, 25-item Likert scale questionnaire, t-test and chi-square analysis. (2 slides with sampling diagram and questionnaire excerpt)
  • Minutes 7–11 (Results): Descriptive statistics table, t-test results (highlight the key t = 5.13 result), chi-square table, logistic regression output. (4 slides, one key result per slide)
  • Minutes 12–13 (Discussion): "The finding that private sector employees perceive more discrimination aligns with the weaker regulatory framework hypothesis. The effect size of d = 0.73 suggests this is not just statistically significant but practically meaningful." (1 slide)
  • Minutes 14–15 (Conclusions): Summary of three key findings, two recommendations, and one direction for future research. (1 slide + Thank you slide)
EXAMPLE 2 — Common Presentation Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Reading slides verbatim: The presenter reads every word on each slide. The audience can read faster than the presenter can speak, so they finish reading and then wait bored. Fix: Use bullet points on slides and elaborate orally. Each slide should have no more than 6 lines of text.

Mistake 2 — Too many slides: A 15-minute presentation with 40 slides means 22 seconds per slide — a rushed, breathless delivery. Fix: Limit to 10–15 slides for a 15-minute presentation (1–1.5 minutes per slide).

Mistake 3 — Tiny text and complex tables: A slide showing a table with 10 rows and 8 columns in 8-point font is unreadable from the back of the room. Fix: Each slide should present at most one key result. Simplify tables to show only the essential numbers. Use a minimum of 20-point font for any text on a slide.

Mistake 4 — No practice: The presenter runs out of time and skips the conclusions. Fix: Practice the full presentation at least 3 times, timing each run. Adjust content until it fits comfortably within the allotted time with a 1–2 minute buffer.

10. Audio-Visual Aids

KEY CONCEPT

Audio-visual aids enhance the clarity, impact, and memorability of a presentation by engaging both the visual and auditory channels of the audience. When used well, they amplify the message; when used poorly, they distract from it.

10.1 Types of Aids

  • Slides (PowerPoint, LaTeX Beamer, Google Slides): The most common format. Use a clean, professional design with consistent colours and fonts. Avoid flashy animations and distracting backgrounds.
  • Whiteboard / Blackboard: Useful for explaining mathematical derivations step by step, allowing the audience to follow the logic in real time.
  • Handouts: Printed summaries distributed to the audience. Useful for detailed tables or complex diagrams that cannot be read from the screen. Ensure they match the slide content.
  • Videos / Animations: Short clips (30–60 seconds) demonstrating a process or phenomenon. Use sparingly and ensure they are cued and tested before the presentation.
  • Physical objects / Models: Rarely used in statistics presentations, but potentially useful for demonstrating quality control principles (e.g., passing around a defective and a non-defective component).

10.2 Design Principles for Slides

  • One idea per slide: Do not cram multiple results onto a single slide.
  • Minimum 20-point font: If the audience cannot read it, do not include it.
  • High contrast: Dark text on light background (or vice versa). Avoid colour combinations that are difficult for colour-blind viewers (red-green).
  • Consistent style: Same font, colours, and layout throughout all slides.
  • Graphs over tables: A well-designed graph communicates trends more effectively than a table of numbers.
  • Annotate key points: Circle or highlight the important number on a graph or table. Do not make the audience search for it.
  • No decorative clip art: Every element should serve a communicative purpose.
EXAMPLE 1 — Effective Use of a Graph Slide

Instead of showing a raw data table of 12 monthly unemployment rates, the presenter displays a line graph with: (a) clearly labelled axes (Month on x-axis, Unemployment Rate % on y-axis); (b) the trend line in bold blue; (c) a horizontal red dashed line marking the national average; (d) an annotation arrow pointing to the peak month ("Oct 2024: 8.3% — Highest in 3 years"); (e) a subtitle: "Unemployment rose sharply after the factory closures in Q3." The audience immediately grasps the trend, the timing, and the reason, which would take three minutes to explain verbally from a table.

EXAMPLE 2 — Ineffective Slide Design and Its Correction

Bad slide: A screenshot of SPSS output showing a full regression table with 8 columns and 15 rows, in 8-point font, with the key p-value buried in the middle. The audience squints and gives up.

Corrected slide: A clean table showing only the 3 predictor variables, their coefficients, standard errors, and p-values in 24-point font. The significant predictors are highlighted in bold. Below the table, one sentence: "Rainfall (p < .001) and Fertiliser (p = .003) are significant predictors; Soil Quality is not (p = .21)." A second slide shows the scatter plot with the regression line and the \(R^2 = 0.78\) annotation. The audience can read and understand the key result in 30 seconds.

11. Presenter's Poise

KEY CONCEPT

Presenter's poise refers to the confidence, composure, and professionalism with which a presenter delivers their talk. Even the most well-prepared content can be undermined by nervous habits, poor body language, or a monotonous voice. Poise is a skill that can be developed through practice and awareness.

11.1 Body Language

  • Posture: Stand upright with shoulders back. Avoid leaning on the podium or slouching. A confident posture projects authority.
  • Eye contact: Look at the audience, not the screen or notes. Make eye contact with different sections of the audience. Reading from notes for more than 20% of the presentation signals lack of preparation.
  • Gestures: Use natural hand gestures to emphasise points. Avoid fidgeting with pens, paper, or clothing. Keep hands out of pockets.
  • Movement: Stand in one place or move purposefully. Pacing back and forth nervously is distracting.

11.2 Voice

  • Volume: Speak loudly enough for the back row to hear. If no microphone, test your volume beforehand.
  • Pace: Speak slightly slower than in conversation. Nervousness causes speeding. Pause at key points to let important results sink in.
  • Tone: Vary your intonation to maintain interest. A monotone puts the audience to sleep. Emphasise key words and results.
  • Filler words: Eliminate "um," "ah," "like," and "you know." Practice will reduce these. Silence is better than a filler word.

11.3 Handling Questions

  • Listen to the entire question before starting to answer.
  • Repeat or rephrase the question so the entire audience hears it.
  • If you don't know the answer, say so honestly: "That's an excellent question. I don't have the data to answer it definitively, but my hypothesis would be…"
  • Don't become defensive. Questions are not attacks — they are requests for clarification.
  • Keep answers concise (30–60 seconds). If the questioner wants more detail, they will follow up.
EXAMPLE 1 — Good Presenter vs. Poor Presenter

Poor presenter: Stands behind the podium, reads from slides in a monotone, never makes eye contact, speaks too fast, fills every pause with "um," and when asked a question, responds defensively with "I already covered that in slide 8."

Good presenter: Stands beside the screen (not behind the podium), faces the audience, makes eye contact with different sections, speaks at a measured pace with natural intonation, pauses after key results ("As you can see, the t-value is 5.13 — well above the critical value. This is a highly significant result."), and when asked a question, says "That's a great question. The reason we used a two-tailed test rather than a one-tailed test is…"

EXAMPLE 2 — Handling a Difficult Question

During a thesis defence, an examiner asks: "Your sample of 200 is quite small. How can you generalise your findings to the entire state?" A defensive response would be: "200 is a reasonable sample size — other studies have used similar sizes." A poised response would be: "Thank you for that important question. You're right that our sample is limited to two cities in Andhra Pradesh, which constrains generalisability. Our power analysis indicated that 200 respondents provide 80% power to detect a medium effect size (d = 0.5) at alpha = 0.05, which is the conventional threshold. However, I fully acknowledge that our findings may not generalise to rural areas or other states. I've noted this as a key limitation in Section 5.4 and suggested a multi-state replication as future research." This response demonstrates honesty, statistical competence, and awareness of limitations — all hallmarks of a mature researcher.
