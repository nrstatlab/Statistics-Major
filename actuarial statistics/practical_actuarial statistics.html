Experiment 1 — Risk Computation for Different Utility Models

PROBLEM

An individual with wealth ₹2 00 000 faces a loss of ₹50 000 with probability 0.2 (else 0). Compute the maximum acceptable premium under three utility functions.

1.1 Linear utility (risk-neutral)

\(u(w) = w\). Maximum premium = \(E(X) = 0.2 \cdot 50000 = ₹10{,}000\).

1.2 Logarithmic utility

\(u(w) = \ln w\). Condition: \(\ln(2{,}00{,}000 - G^*) = 0.8 \ln(2{,}00{,}000) + 0.2 \ln(1{,}50{,}000)\).

RHS = 0.8(12.2061) + 0.2(11.9184) = 9.7649 + 2.3837 = 12.1486.

\(2{,}00{,}000 - G^* = e^{12.1486} = 1{,}89{,}435\). Hence \(G^* = ₹10{,}565\).

Risk premium = 10565 − 10000 = ₹565.

1.3 Exponential utility

\(u(w) = -e^{-aw}\) with \(a = 0.00002\). Condition:

\(-e^{-0.00002 (2{,}00{,}000 - G^*)} = 0.8(-e^{-0.00002 \cdot 2{,}00{,}000}) + 0.2(-e^{-0.00002 \cdot 1{,}50{,}000})\)

RHS = \(-0.8 e^{-4} - 0.2 e^{-3} = -0.01466 - 0.00996 = -0.02462\).

\(-e^{-0.00002 (2{,}00{,}000 - G^*)} = -0.02462\) ⇒ \(0.00002(2{,}00{,}000 - G^*) = -\ln(0.02462) = 3.7044\).

\(2{,}00{,}000 - G^* = 185{,}220\); \(G^* = ₹14{,}780\). Highest premium of the three (most risk-averse).

Comparison: Linear ₹10 000 (pure premium), Log ₹10 565 (mild aversion), Exp ₹14 780 (strong aversion).

Experiment 2 — Discrete and Continuous Risk Calculations

2.1 Discrete

Annual claim count \(N \sim\) Poisson(5); each claim is ₹10 000 (constant size). Find \(E(S),\; \text{Var}(S)\).

\(S = 10000 \cdot N\). \(E(S) = 10000 \cdot 5 = ₹50{,}000\); \(\text{Var}(S) = 10000^2 \cdot 5 = 5 \times 10^8\); \(\sigma_S = ₹22{,}361\).

2.2 Continuous

Claim size \(X \sim\) Exponential with mean ₹20 000. 10 independent policies. Compute \(E(S),\; \text{Var}(S)\) and probability \(S >\) ₹2 50 000.

Each \(X\): mean = SD = ₹20 000. Sum of 10 iid Exp(λ) = Gamma(10, λ) with mean ₹2 00 000 and variance \(10 \cdot 20000^2 = 4 \times 10^9\), SD = ₹63 246.

By CLT: \(P(S > 250000) \approx P(Z > (250000 - 200000)/63246) = P(Z > 0.79) = 0.215\).

Experiment 3 — Collective Risk Model (Aggregate Claims)

PROBLEM

In the collective risk model \(S = X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_N\) where \(N\) is random (number of claims) and the \(X_i\) (claim sizes) are iid and independent of N. Compute mean and variance of \(S\).

Compound formulas:

\(E(S) = E(N) \cdot E(X)\)

\(\text{Var}(S) = E(N) \cdot \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(N) \cdot [E(X)]^2\) (law of total variance).

Compound Poisson (most common): \(N \sim\) Poisson(λ), so \(E(N) = \text{Var}(N) = \lambda\).

Then: \(E(S) = \lambda E(X);\; \text{Var}(S) = \lambda \cdot E(X^2)\).

Numerical example

\(\lambda = 100,\; X \sim\) Exponential with mean 5000. Then \(E(X) = 5000,\; E(X^2) = 2 \cdot 5000^2 = 5 \times 10^7\).

\(E(S) = 100 \cdot 5000 = ₹5{,}00{,}000\); \(\text{Var}(S) = 100 \cdot 5 \times 10^7 = 5 \times 10^9\); \(\sigma_S = ₹70{,}711\).

Premium with safety loading 20 %: \(P = 1.2 \cdot E(S) = ₹6{,}00{,}000\).

\(P(S > P) \approx P(Z > (600000 - 500000)/70711) = P(Z > 1.41) = 0.079\).

Experiment 4 — Individual Risk Model (Aggregate Claims)

PROBLEM

A portfolio of 200 policies, each pays ₹1 00 000 on claim with probability 0.01. Compute \(E(S),\; \text{Var}(S)\), and a 95% upper bound for premium.

Each \(X_i = 100000 \cdot I_i\), \(I_i \sim\) Bernoulli(0.01).

\(E(X_i) = 1000;\;\) \(\text{Var}(X_i) = 100000^2 \cdot 0.01 \cdot 0.99 = 9.9 \times 10^7\).

\(E(S) = 200 \cdot 1000 = ₹2{,}00{,}000\); \(\text{Var}(S) = 200 \cdot 9.9 \times 10^7 = 1.98 \times 10^{10}\); \(\sigma_S = ₹1{,}40{,}712\).

95% premium: \(P = E(S) + 1.645 \cdot \sigma_S = 200000 + 1.645 \cdot 140712 = 200000 + 231471 = ₹4{,}31{,}471\).

Experiment 5 — Ruin Probabilities and Aggregate Losses

The insurer's surplus process at time \(t\):

\(U(t) = u + c t - S(t)\), where \(u\) is initial surplus, \(c\) is premium income rate, \(S(t)\) is aggregate claims by time \(t\).

Ruin occurs if \(U(t) < 0\) for some \(t\). Ultimate ruin probability: \(\psi(u) = P(U(t) < 0 \text{ some } t \ge 0)\).

Lundberg's Inequality

For compound Poisson surplus with safety loading and finite MGF:

\(\psi(u) \le e^{-R u}\), where \(R\) is the adjustment coefficient satisfying:

\(\lambda M_X(R) - \lambda - c R = 0\).

Example (Exponential claims)

\(\lambda = 100,\; X \sim\) Exp(1/5000) (mean 5000). Premium loading \(\theta = 0.2\), so \(c = 1.2 \lambda E(X) = 1.2 \cdot 100 \cdot 5000 = 6{,}00{,}000\) per unit time.

For exponential claims, the ruin probability has a closed form:

\(\psi(u) = \dfrac{1}{1+\theta} e^{-\theta u / [(1+\theta) E(X)]}\).

With \(\theta = 0.2,\; E(X) = 5000\): \(\psi(u) = (1/1.2) e^{-0.2 u/(1.2 \cdot 5000)} = 0.833 \, e^{-u/30000}\).

If \(u = 30000\): \(\psi(30000) = 0.833 \cdot e^{-1} = 0.306\). Long-run ruin probability ~31 %.

If \(u = 100000\): \(\psi = 0.833 \cdot e^{-3.33} = 0.030\) — much safer.

Experiment 6 — Annuity and Present Value of Contract

6.1 Annuity Certain

An annuity pays ₹1 lakh at the end of each year for 20 years. Interest \(i = 6\%\). Compute the present value.

\(v = 1/1.06 = 0.9434;\;\) \(a_{\overline{20}|} = (1 - 0.9434^{20})/0.06 = (1 - 0.3118)/0.06 = 11.4699\).

PV = 100000 × 11.4699 = ₹11 47 000.

6.2 Whole-Life Annuity

(\(x = 60\)) with \(\ddot a_{60} = 12.5\) at \(i = 5\%\). Annual pension ₹50 000.

APV = 50000 × 12.5 = ₹6 25 000.

6.3 Monthly Annuity Approximation

\(\ddot a_{60}^{(12)} \approx \ddot a_{60} - 11/24 = 12.5 - 0.458 = 12.042\).

If pension is ₹50000/year = ₹4167/month, lump sum = 50000 × 12.042 = ₹6 02 100.

6.4 Continuous Annuity

\(\bar a_{60} \approx \ddot a_{60} - 0.5 = 12.0\).

PV of ₹50 000/year continuous = ₹6 00 000.

Experiment 7 — Computing Premium for Different Insurance Schemes

Assume from a life table at \(i = 5\%\), age \(x = 30\):

\(A_{30} = 0.105,\; \ddot a_{30} = 18.85,\; A^{\,1}_{30:\overline{20}|} = 0.022,\; \ddot a_{30:\overline{20}|} = 12.93,\; {}_{20}E_{30} = 0.355\).

7.1 Whole-Life Insurance (face ₹10 lakh)

Net annual premium = \(10{,}00{,}000 \cdot 0.105 / 18.85 = ₹5{,}570\).

7.2 20-Year Term Insurance (face ₹10 lakh)

NSP = \(10{,}00{,}000 \cdot 0.022 = ₹22{,}000\). Net annual premium = \(22000/12.93 = ₹1{,}701\). Much cheaper than whole-life because no benefit on survival.

7.3 20-Year Endowment Insurance (face ₹10 lakh)

\(A_{30:\overline{20}|} = 0.022 + 0.355 = 0.377\). NSP = \(10{,}00{,}000 \cdot 0.377 = ₹3{,}77{,}000\).

Net annual premium = \(377000/12.93 = ₹29{,}157\). The high premium reflects guaranteed payout at maturity.

7.4 10-Year Limited-Payment Whole-Life

Suppose \(\ddot a_{30:\overline{10}|} = 7.87\). Annual premium = \(10{,}00{,}000 \cdot 0.105 / 7.87 = ₹13{,}342\). About 2.4× the ordinary whole-life premium.

\(_{10}p_{50} = l_{60}/l_{50} = 85000/92300 = 0.9209\) — probability life-50 survives to age 60.

\(_{20}q_{30} = 1 - l_{50}/l_{30} = 1 - 92300/97500 = 0.0533\) — probability of dying in next 20 years.

8.2 Force of Mortality from Life Table (Approximation)

\(\mu_{60} \approx -\ln(p_{60}) = -\ln(0.98588) = 0.01421\) per year (under constant-force assumption within the year).

8.3 Gompertz Fit

Suppose \(\mu(50) = 0.005\) and \(\mu(70) = 0.030\). Fit \(\mu(x) = Bc^x\):

\(c^{70}/c^{50} = c^{20} = 0.030/0.005 = 6\); \(c = 6^{1/20} = 1.0937\).

\(B = 0.005 / c^{50} = 0.005 / 91.41 = 5.47 \times 10^{-5}\).

So \(\mu(x) = 5.47 \times 10^{-5} \times 1.0937^x\). Compare with Makeham by adding constant term \(A\) if accidental risk is significant.

8.4 Computing \(\bar A_x\) under Gompertz

No closed form; compute numerically using \(\bar A_x = \int_0^\infty v^t\, {}_tp_x\, \mu(x+t)\, dt\) with quadrature (Simpson's rule, etc.). Typical practical setup uses spreadsheet or R for the integration.
