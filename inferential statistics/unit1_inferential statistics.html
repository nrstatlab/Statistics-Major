Unit 1 — Theory of Estimation

Estimator vs estimate; properties of a good estimator; method of moments & MLE; Rao–Cramer lower bound; confidence intervals.

Topics Covered

Estimator vs Estimate Unbiasedness Consistency Efficiency Sufficiency Method of Moments MLE Rao-Cramer Confidence Intervals

1. Estimator and Estimate

DEFINITIONS
  • Parameter (\(\theta\)): a numerical characteristic of a population (e.g. mean \(\mu\), variance \(\sigma^2\)). Usually unknown.
  • Statistic: a function of the sample (no unknowns).
  • Estimator (\(\hat\theta\)): a statistic used to estimate the parameter.
  • Estimate: the numerical value of the estimator computed from a particular sample.

Two kinds of estimation:

  • Point estimation — single value (e.g. \(\bar X\) for \(\mu\)).
  • Interval estimation — an interval likely to contain \(\theta\) (confidence interval).

2. Criteria of a Good Estimator

2.1 Unbiasedness

\(\hat\theta\) is unbiased for \(\theta\) if \(E(\hat\theta) = \theta\) for all \(\theta\).

EXAMPLE 1

Sample mean \(\bar X = \frac{1}{n}\sum X_i\) is unbiased for \(\mu\): \(E(\bar X) = \mu\).

EXAMPLE 2

Sample variance \(s^2 = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum(X_i - \bar X)^2\) is unbiased for \(\sigma^2\). The naïve version with \(1/n\) is biased: \(E(\sigma_n^2) = \frac{n-1}{n}\sigma^2\).

2.2 Consistency

\(\hat\theta_n\) is consistent if \(\hat\theta_n \xrightarrow{P} \theta\) as \(n \to \infty\). By Chebyshev: an unbiased estimator is consistent if \(\text{Var}(\hat\theta_n) \to 0\).

EXAMPLE 1

\(\bar X\) is consistent for \(\mu\): unbiased and \(\text{Var}(\bar X) = \sigma^2/n \to 0\).

EXAMPLE 2

The sample proportion \(\hat p = X/n\) (where \(X\) is the count of successes in Bernoulli trials) is consistent for \(p\) with variance \(p(1-p)/n \to 0\).

2.3 Efficiency

Among unbiased estimators, the one with minimum variance is most efficient. Relative efficiency:

\[ \text{RE}(\hat\theta_1, \hat\theta_2) = \dfrac{\text{Var}(\hat\theta_2)}{\text{Var}(\hat\theta_1)}. \]

If RE > 1, \(\hat\theta_1\) is more efficient than \(\hat\theta_2\).

EXAMPLE 1

For a normal population, both \(\bar X\) and the sample median are unbiased for \(\mu\). \(\text{Var}(\bar X) = \sigma^2/n\), \(\text{Var}(\text{median}) \approx \pi\sigma^2/(2n) \approx 1.57\,\sigma^2/n\). Hence \(\bar X\) is about 57 % more efficient.

EXAMPLE 2

If \(T_1\) has variance 5 and \(T_2\) has variance 8 (both unbiased), \(\text{RE}(T_1,T_2) = 8/5 = 1.6\), so \(T_1\) is 60 % more efficient.

2.4 Sufficiency

A statistic \(T\) is sufficient for \(\theta\) if the conditional distribution of the sample given \(T\) does not depend on \(\theta\). It captures all the information in the data about \(\theta\).

Fisher–Neyman Factorisation Theorem

\(T(\mathbf X)\) is sufficient for \(\theta\) iff the joint pdf factors as

\[ f(\mathbf x; \theta) \;=\; g(T(\mathbf x); \theta) \cdot h(\mathbf x), \]

where \(h\) does not depend on \(\theta\).

EXAMPLE 1

For Bernoulli\((p)\) sample, \(\sum X_i\) is sufficient for \(p\). Joint pmf factors as \(p^{\sum x_i}(1-p)^{n-\sum x_i}\).

EXAMPLE 2

For \(N(\mu, \sigma^2)\) with known \(\sigma^2\), \(\sum X_i\) (or \(\bar X\)) is sufficient for \(\mu\).

3. Methods of Estimation

3.1 Method of Moments (MoM)

Equate population moments \(\mu'_r\) with sample moments \(m'_r\) and solve for parameters.

\[ m'_r \;=\; \dfrac{1}{n}\sum X_i^r \;=\; \mu'_r(\theta). \]
EXAMPLE 1 (Exponential)

For \(X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)\), \(\mu'_1 = 1/\lambda\). MoM: \(\bar X = 1/\hat\lambda\) ⇒ \(\hat\lambda = 1/\bar X\).

EXAMPLE 2 (Uniform)

For \(X \sim U(0, \theta)\), \(\mu'_1 = \theta/2\). MoM: \(\hat\theta = 2 \bar X\). (Often biased; MLE \(\max(X_i)\) is better.)

3.2 Method of Maximum Likelihood (MLE)

The likelihood function is \(L(\theta) = \prod f(x_i; \theta)\). The MLE \(\hat\theta\) maximises \(L\) (or equivalently \(\log L\)).

Properties of MLE

  1. Consistent.
  2. Asymptotically normal: \(\sqrt n(\hat\theta - \theta) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, 1/I(\theta))\).
  3. Asymptotically efficient (achieves Rao–Cramer lower bound).
  4. Invariant: if \(\hat\theta\) is MLE of \(\theta\), then \(g(\hat\theta)\) is MLE of \(g(\theta)\).
  5. Sufficient (a function of a sufficient statistic).

MLE for Standard Distributions

  • Bernoulli/Binomial(n, p): \(\hat p = X/n = \bar X\) (for Bernoulli).
  • Poisson(λ): \(\hat\lambda = \bar X\).
  • Normal(μ, σ²) with both unknown: \(\hat\mu = \bar X,\;\; \hat\sigma^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum(X_i - \bar X)^2\) (the divisor is \(n\), not \(n-1\)).
EXAMPLE 1 (Bernoulli)

\(L(p) = p^{\sum x_i}(1-p)^{n-\sum x_i}\). \(\frac{d}{dp}\log L = \sum x_i / p - (n - \sum x_i)/(1-p) = 0\) ⇒ \(\hat p = \bar X\).

EXAMPLE 2 (Normal mean, σ known)

\(\log L = -\frac{n}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2) - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum(x_i - \mu)^2\). \(\partial/\partial\mu\) = 0 gives \(\hat\mu = \bar X\).

4. Rao–Cramer Inequality

STATEMENT

For an unbiased estimator \(\hat\theta\) of \(\theta\) (under regularity conditions),

\[ \text{Var}(\hat\theta) \;\ge\; \dfrac{1}{n\, I(\theta)}, \]

where the Fisher information is

\[ I(\theta) \;=\; E\!\left[\left(\dfrac{\partial \log f(X;\theta)}{\partial \theta}\right)^2\right] \;=\; -E\!\left[\dfrac{\partial^2 \log f(X;\theta)}{\partial \theta^2}\right]. \]

Properties

  1. Defines the minimum possible variance for any unbiased estimator (the "lower bound").
  2. An estimator that attains the bound is called most efficient (MVUE).
  3. MLEs asymptotically attain this bound.
EXAMPLE 1 (Bernoulli)

\(I(p) = 1/[p(1-p)]\). Lower bound = \(p(1-p)/n\). Variance of \(\bar X\) (for Bernoulli) = \(p(1-p)/n\) — bound attained.

EXAMPLE 2 (Normal mean, σ known)

\(I(\mu) = 1/\sigma^2\). Lower bound = \(\sigma^2/n\). Variance of \(\bar X\) = \(\sigma^2/n\) — attained.

5. Confidence Intervals (CIs)

DEFINITION

A \((1-\alpha)100\%\) confidence interval for \(\theta\) is a random interval \([L, U]\) such that

\[ P(L \le \theta \le U) \;=\; 1 - \alpha. \]

Common confidence levels: 90 % (\(z = 1.645\)), 95 % (\(z = 1.96\)), 99 % (\(z = 2.576\)).

Standard CI Formulas

  • Population mean (σ known): \(\bar X \pm z_{\alpha/2}\, \sigma/\sqrt n\).
  • Population mean (σ unknown, small n): \(\bar X \pm t_{\alpha/2, n-1}\, s/\sqrt n\).
  • Population proportion: \(\hat p \pm z_{\alpha/2}\sqrt{\hat p(1-\hat p)/n}\).
  • Difference of means (independent samples, σ's known): \((\bar X_1 - \bar X_2) \pm z_{\alpha/2}\sqrt{\sigma_1^2/n_1 + \sigma_2^2/n_2}\).
  • Population variance (normal data): \(\Big[\dfrac{(n-1)s^2}{\chi^2_{\alpha/2,n-1}},\; \dfrac{(n-1)s^2}{\chi^2_{1-\alpha/2,n-1}}\Big]\).
EXAMPLE 1 (Mean, σ known)

Sample of 64 light bulbs has \(\bar X = 1\,250\) hours; \(\sigma = 80\). 95 % CI for \(\mu\):

\(1\,250 \pm 1.96 (80/\sqrt{64}) = 1\,250 \pm 19.6 = (1\,230.4,\; 1\,269.6)\) hours.

EXAMPLE 2 (Proportion)

Out of 400 voters, 240 favour candidate A. \(\hat p = 0.6\). 95 % CI:

\(0.6 \pm 1.96\sqrt{0.6 \cdot 0.4 / 400} = 0.6 \pm 0.048 = (0.552,\; 0.648)\).

Key Take-aways

  • Estimator is a rule; estimate is its value for a specific sample.
  • Four properties of a good estimator: U–C–E–S (Unbiased, Consistent, Efficient, Sufficient).
  • MoM matches sample moments to population moments; MLE maximises likelihood.
  • MLEs are consistent, asymptotically normal, efficient and invariant.
  • Rao–Cramer gives the minimum-variance lower bound for unbiased estimators.
  • CI = point estimate ± (critical value) × SE.
