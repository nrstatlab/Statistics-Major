Unit 3 — Large Sample Tests (Z-tests)

Z-tests for single mean, difference of means, proportions, standard deviations and correlation coefficient. Confidence intervals for means.

Topics Covered

Single Mean Difference of Means Single Proportion Difference of Proportions SD Tests Correlation Test CIs

1. When are Large Sample Tests Used?

When the sample size \(n\) is large (rule of thumb: \(n > 30\)) and the population SD \(\sigma\) is known (or can be reliably estimated by \(s\)), the sampling distribution of any statistic that is a sum or average is approximately normal by the Central Limit Theorem.

Hence we use the standard normal \(Z\)-statistic for testing.

2. Z-test for Single Mean

\(H_0: \mu = \mu_0\). Test statistic:

\[ Z \;=\; \dfrac{\bar X - \mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt n} \]

If \(\sigma\) is unknown but \(n\) large, replace \(\sigma\) by sample SD \(s\).

Under \(H_0\), \(Z \sim N(0, 1)\).

Confidence Interval for μ

\[ \bar X \;\pm\; z_{\alpha/2}\cdot \dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}. \]
EXAMPLE 1

A drug company claims their tablets weigh on average 500 mg with σ = 6 mg. A sample of 64 tablets gives \(\bar X = 498.5\) mg. Test at 5 %.

\(Z = (498.5 - 500)/(6/8) = -2.0\). \(|Z| = 2.0 > 1.96\) ⇒ reject \(H_0\). The mean weight differs significantly.

EXAMPLE 2

A school principal claims average IQ = 100 (σ = 15). Sample of 100 students has \(\bar X = 104\). Test if IQ is higher (one-tailed).

\(Z = (104 - 100)/(15/10) = 2.67 > 1.645\) ⇒ reject \(H_0\) at 5 %. Evidence that mean IQ > 100.

3. Z-test for Difference of Two Means

\(H_0: \mu_1 = \mu_2\). Independent samples of sizes \(n_1, n_2\) (both large) with means \(\bar X_1, \bar X_2\) and SDs \(\sigma_1, \sigma_2\). Test statistic:

\[ Z \;=\; \dfrac{\bar X_1 - \bar X_2}{\sqrt{\sigma_1^2/n_1 + \sigma_2^2/n_2}} \;\sim\; N(0, 1). \]

CI for μ₁ − μ₂

\[ (\bar X_1 - \bar X_2) \;\pm\; z_{\alpha/2}\sqrt{\dfrac{\sigma_1^2}{n_1} + \dfrac{\sigma_2^2}{n_2}}. \]
EXAMPLE 1

Boys: \(n_1 = 100,\; \bar X_1 = 165\) cm, \(\sigma_1 = 5\). Girls: \(n_2 = 80,\; \bar X_2 = 158\) cm, \(\sigma_2 = 4\).

\(Z = (165 - 158)/\sqrt{25/100 + 16/80} = 7/\sqrt{0.45} = 10.43\) ⇒ highly significant.

EXAMPLE 2

Two methods of teaching: \(n_1 = 50,\bar X_1 = 72,\sigma_1 = 8\); \(n_2 = 60,\bar X_2 = 75,\sigma_2 = 7\). Test at 5 %.

\(Z = (72 - 75)/\sqrt{64/50 + 49/60} = -3/\sqrt{2.097} = -2.07\). \(|Z| > 1.96\) ⇒ reject \(H_0\).

4. Z-test for Single Proportion

\(H_0: p = p_0\). Sample proportion \(\hat p = X/n\). Under \(H_0\):

\[ Z \;=\; \dfrac{\hat p - p_0}{\sqrt{p_0(1-p_0)/n}} \;\sim\; N(0, 1). \]

CI for p

\[ \hat p \;\pm\; z_{\alpha/2}\sqrt{\hat p(1-\hat p)/n}. \]
EXAMPLE 1

Out of 1000 customers, 540 prefer brand A. Test \(H_0: p = 0.5\) at 5 %.

\(\hat p = 0.54\). \(Z = (0.54 - 0.5)/\sqrt{0.5(0.5)/1000} = 0.04/0.01581 = 2.53\) ⇒ reject \(H_0\). Significant majority for A.

EXAMPLE 2

A coin is tossed 400 times yielding 230 heads. Test fairness.

\(\hat p = 0.575\). \(Z = (0.575 - 0.5)/\sqrt{0.25/400} = 0.075/0.025 = 3.0\) ⇒ reject \(H_0\). Coin is biased.

5. Z-test for Difference of Two Proportions

\(H_0: p_1 = p_2\). Pooled proportion \(\hat p = (X_1 + X_2)/(n_1 + n_2)\). Test statistic:

\[ Z \;=\; \dfrac{\hat p_1 - \hat p_2}{\sqrt{\hat p(1-\hat p)\left(\dfrac{1}{n_1} + \dfrac{1}{n_2}\right)}}. \]
EXAMPLE 1

Town A: 60 of 200 favoured proposal. Town B: 90 of 250. Test \(H_0: p_1 = p_2\).

\(\hat p_1 = 0.30,\; \hat p_2 = 0.36;\; \hat p = 150/450 = 1/3\). SE = \(\sqrt{(1/3)(2/3)(1/200 + 1/250)} = 0.0447\).

\(Z = -0.06/0.0447 = -1.34 < 1.96\) ⇒ do not reject; no significant difference.

EXAMPLE 2

Defective rates of two factories: F1: 50 defective in 500; F2: 30 in 400. Test \(p_1 = p_2\).

\(\hat p_1 = 0.10, \hat p_2 = 0.075, \hat p = 80/900 = 0.0889\). SE = \(\sqrt{0.0889 \cdot 0.9111(1/500 + 1/400)} = 0.0190\).

\(Z = 0.025/0.019 = 1.32\) ⇒ not significant at 5 %.

6. Z-test for Standard Deviation

6.1 Single SD

For large \(n\), the sampling distribution of sample SD \(s\) is approximately \(N(\sigma,\; \sigma^2/2n)\).

\[ Z \;=\; \dfrac{s - \sigma_0}{\sigma_0/\sqrt{2n}} \;\sim\; N(0, 1). \]

6.2 Difference of Two SDs

\[ Z \;=\; \dfrac{s_1 - s_2}{\sqrt{\sigma_1^2/(2n_1) + \sigma_2^2/(2n_2)}}. \]
EXAMPLE 1

From a sample of 200, \(s = 14.5\); test \(H_0: \sigma = 15\).

\(Z = (14.5 - 15)/(15/\sqrt{400}) = -0.5/0.75 = -0.67\) ⇒ not significant at 5 %.

EXAMPLE 2

\(n_1 = 100,\; s_1 = 8;\; n_2 = 150,\; s_2 = 6\). \(Z = (8-6)/\sqrt{64/200 + 36/300} = 2/\sqrt{0.44} = 3.02\). Reject \(H_0\).

7. Z-test for Correlation Coefficient

To test \(H_0: \rho = 0\) (no correlation in the population), use:

\[ Z \;=\; r\sqrt{n - 2}/\sqrt{1 - r^2}\;\;\text{(for }H_0: \rho = 0)\!. \]

(Strictly a t-statistic with \(n-2\) df, but for large \(n\) treated as Z.)

Fisher's Z-transformation (for \(\rho = \rho_0 \ne 0\))

\[ Z' = \dfrac{1}{2}\ln\!\left(\dfrac{1 + r}{1 - r}\right) \;\sim\; N\!\left(\dfrac{1}{2}\ln\!\left(\dfrac{1+\rho_0}{1-\rho_0}\right),\; \dfrac{1}{n - 3}\right). \]

Test statistic: \(Z = (Z' - \zeta_0)\sqrt{n - 3}\) where \(\zeta_0 = \frac{1}{2}\ln((1+\rho_0)/(1-\rho_0))\).

EXAMPLE 1 (\(\rho = 0\))

\(r = 0.4,\; n = 100\). Test \(H_0: \rho = 0\). \(Z = 0.4\sqrt{98}/\sqrt{0.84} = 4.32\) ⇒ highly significant.

EXAMPLE 2 (\(\rho = 0.5\))

\(r = 0.7,\; n = 28\). Test \(H_0: \rho = 0.5\) using Fisher's Z.

\(Z' = 0.5\ln(1.7/0.3) = 0.867\). \(\zeta_0 = 0.5\ln(1.5/0.5) = 0.549\). \(Z = (0.867 - 0.549)\sqrt{25} = 1.59\) ⇒ not significant at 5 %.

Key Take-aways

  • Large sample (n > 30) ⇒ use Z-test thanks to CLT.
  • All test statistics have the form (estimate − hypothesized value) / standard error.
  • CI = estimate ± zα/2 × SE.
  • For correlation \(\rho = 0\): use \(t\)-statistic (treated as Z for large n).
  • For correlation \(\rho \ne 0\): use Fisher's Z-transformation.
