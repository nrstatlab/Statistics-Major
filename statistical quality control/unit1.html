1. What is Quality?

DEFINITION

Quality in the industrial context means the degree to which a product or service fulfils the customer's requirements. It is not an abstract ideal — it is a measurable attribute that can be quantified, monitored, and improved through statistical methods.

The Japanese quality guru Genichi Taguchi defined quality as "the loss a product causes to society after being shipped" — meaning any deviation from the target specification is a quality loss, even if the product still passes inspection.

Dimensions of Quality

  • Performance: primary operating characteristics (speed, strength, accuracy).
  • Reliability: probability of functioning without failure over time.
  • Conformance: meeting design specifications and tolerances.
  • Durability: total usable life before replacement is needed.
  • Consistency: uniformity of output — every unit identical to the next.

Statistical Quality Control (SQC) is primarily concerned with conformance and consistency — ensuring that every item produced stays within specification and that variation is minimised.

2. Importance of Statistical Quality Control

WHY SQC?

SQC provides a scientific, data-driven framework for monitoring and improving quality. It replaces the old "inspect at the end and reject the bad ones" philosophy with a prevention-oriented approach — detecting problems as they arise and fixing them before large volumes of defective output accumulate.

Key Benefits

  1. Early detection: Control charts signal a process shift within hours, not after a whole batch is produced.
  2. Cost reduction: Rework, scrap, warranty claims, and customer returns all decrease.
  3. Customer satisfaction: Consistent quality builds brand loyalty.
  4. Process understanding: Studying variation reveals what drives the process.
  5. Competitive advantage: ISO 9001, Six-Sigma and other certifications require SQC evidence.

3. The 4 M's of SQC

FRAMEWORK

Any industrial process has four broad input categories that collectively determine output quality. These are known as the 4 M's:

M FactorWhat it CoversExample of Influence on Quality
ManSkill, training, motivation, fatigue of operatorsAn untrained operator may set machine incorrectly, causing out-of-spec output.
MachineEquipment condition, calibration, wear & tearA worn bearing causes excess vibration → dimensional variation in parts.
MaterialRaw material properties, supplier consistency, storageSteel with inconsistent carbon content leads to variable hardness after heat treatment.
MethodProcess parameters, SOPs, temperature, speed, sequenceCuring temperature too high → paint blisters; too low → paint does not adhere.

The 4 M's framework helps trace the root cause of a quality problem. When a control chart signals an out-of-control condition, the engineer checks each M in turn: Was it a new operator (Man)? Did a tool wear out (Machine)? Was a different batch of raw material used (Material)? Was a process recipe changed (Method)?

EXAMPLE 1 — Machine-related quality issue

A CNC lathe produces shafts with a nominal diameter of 25.000 mm. Over three months the shaft diameters slowly drift upward from 25.000 to 25.015 mm. Investigation reveals the cutting tool is wearing (Machine). After tool replacement, diameters return to 25.000 mm. This is an assignable cause — the tool wear — that was detected and corrected.

EXAMPLE 2 — Material-related quality issue

A pharmaceutical company produces tablets of 500 mg. Suddenly the tablet weights jump from 498–502 mg to 510–518 mg. The control chart signals out-of-control. Investigation reveals a new supplier's active ingredient powder has a different bulk density (Material), causing the automatic filling machine to dispense more mass. Switching back to the original supplier's specification resolves the problem.

4. Causes of Variation

FUNDAMENTAL PRINCIPLE

No two items are identical. Even in the most carefully controlled process, there will be variation. SQC distinguishes two fundamentally different types:

4.1 Chance (Random) Causes of Variation

  • Also called common causes or unavoidable variation.
  • Inherent in the process — always present, small in magnitude.
  • Examples: minor temperature fluctuations, tiny raw-material composition differences, micro-vibrations, operator eye–hand variability.
  • Effect on output: stable, predictable pattern around a fixed mean.
  • A process with only chance causes is said to be in statistical control.
  • Eliminating chance causes is usually not economically justified — the process must be fundamentally redesigned.

4.2 Assignable (Special) Causes of Variation

  • Also called special causes or preventable variation.
  • Not part of the random system — arises from a specific, identifiable source.
  • Examples: tool breakage, wrong machine setting, new untrained operator, defective raw-material batch, power surge.
  • Effect on output: shift, trend, or sudden spike in the process mean or spread.
  • A process with one or more active assignable causes is out of statistical control.
  • Finding and removing assignable causes is the primary goal of SQC — it is economically justified and often urgent.

EXAMPLE 1 — Chance cause

Bottles are filled with 1000 mL of juice by an automatic filling machine. Measurements on 50 bottles range from 998.2 to 1001.6 mL, with mean 1000.1 mL and SD 0.9 mL. The variation is small, random, and symmetric — no single cause can be identified. This is chance variation. The process is in control.

EXAMPLE 2 — Assignable cause

The same filling machine suddenly starts producing bottles with fill volumes of 1012–1018 mL. The \(\bar{X}\) chart shows a point above the UCL. Investigation reveals the filling nozzle is partially clogged, causing back-pressure and overfilling (Machine/Method). This is an assignable cause. After cleaning the nozzle the process returns to the 998–1002 mL range.

5. Process Control versus Product Control

DISTINCTION

SQC has two complementary arms that address quality at different stages of production:

5.1 Process Control

  • When: During production — real-time monitoring.
  • Tool: Control charts (\(\bar{X}\), R, p, c, etc.).
  • Goal: Detect and eliminate assignable causes before large quantities of defective items accumulate.
  • Philosophy: Prevention is better than cure.
  • Analogy: Monitoring blood pressure regularly to prevent a health crisis.

5.2 Product Control

  • When: After a batch or lot is produced — accept/reject decision.
  • Tool: Acceptance sampling plans (single, double, multiple, sequential).
  • Goal: Decide whether a submitted lot meets quality standards, based on inspecting a sample.
  • Philosophy: Sort the good lots from the bad ones — a screening gate.
  • Analogy: Taking a blood test and then deciding on treatment.

EXAMPLE 1 — Process control scenario

A steel-rolling mill monitors the thickness of steel sheets every 30 minutes using an \(\bar{X}\)-R chart. When a point falls above the UCL, the line is stopped and the roll gap is adjusted. This is process control — the focus is on keeping the rolling process stable and preventing further defective sheets.

EXAMPLE 2 — Product control scenario

A supplier ships 10 000 bolts to a customer. The customer inspects a random sample of 200 bolts; if 5 or fewer are defective, the lot is accepted; otherwise it is rejected and returned. This is product control — the focus is on deciding whether the submitted lot is acceptable, not on monitoring the supplier's production line.

6. Statistical Basis of Shewhart Control Charts

ORIGIN

Walter A. Shewhart of Bell Telephone Laboratories introduced the control chart in 1924. His insight: if a process is influenced only by chance causes, the quality characteristic follows a stable probability distribution (often approximately normal). Any observation that falls far from the centre — beyond what this distribution predicts — signals an assignable cause.

6.1 Three-Sigma Limits

Shewhart placed control limits at 3 standard deviations from the process mean:

\[ \text{UCL} = \mu + 3\sigma, \qquad \text{CL} = \mu, \qquad \text{LCL} = \mu - 3\sigma. \]

Under normality, the probability of a point falling outside the 3\(\sigma\) limits by chance alone is approximately 0.27% (0.135% in each tail). So if a point does fall outside, we have strong evidence that the process mean has shifted — an assignable cause is likely present.

6.2 Type I and Type II Errors in Control Charts

  • Type I error (\(\alpha\)): Searching for an assignable cause when none exists (false alarm). With 3\(\sigma\) limits, \(\alpha\) ≈ 0.0027 per sample — very low.
  • Type II error (\(\beta\)): Failing to detect a real shift. Depends on the magnitude of the shift.
  • Wider limits reduce \(\alpha\) but increase \(\beta\); narrower limits reduce \(\beta\) but increase \(\alpha\). The 3\(\sigma\) convention is a widely accepted balance.

6.3 General Structure of a Control Chart

A Shewhart control chart has three horizontal lines plotted against the sample number (or time):

  1. Centre Line (CL): the expected value of the statistic being plotted.
  2. Upper Control Limit (UCL): the maximum acceptable value.
  3. Lower Control Limit (LCL): the minimum acceptable value.
\[ \text{UCL} = \mu_{\text{stat}} + 3\,\sigma_{\text{stat}}, \qquad \text{CL} = \mu_{\text{stat}}, \qquad \text{LCL} = \mu_{\text{stat}} - 3\,\sigma_{\text{stat}}. \]

The subscript "stat" refers to the sample statistic being plotted (sample mean, range, proportion, count, etc.).

6.4 Interpreting a Control Chart

A process is in control when:

  • All points lie between LCL and UCL.
  • No systematic pattern (runs, trends, cycles) exists among the points.

A process is out of control if any of the following occur:

  • One or more points outside the control limits.
  • A run of 7 or more consecutive points on the same side of the CL.
  • A trend of 6 or more consecutive increasing or decreasing points.
  • 2 out of 3 consecutive points beyond 2\(\sigma\) on the same side.
  • A systematic cycle or periodic pattern.
EXAMPLE 1 — 3\(\sigma\) limits in action

A process fills packets with mean weight \(\mu\) = 500 g and \(\sigma\) = 5 g. The 3\(\sigma\) limits for individual measurements are UCL = 500 + 15 = 515 g and LCL = 500 − 15 = 485 g. A sample packet weighs 518 g — above the UCL. Conclusion: an assignable cause is likely present; investigate the filling mechanism.

EXAMPLE 2 — Run pattern signalling an out-of-control process

On an \(\bar{X}\) chart for shaft diameter, no individual point falls outside the 3\(\sigma\) limits. However, the last 8 consecutive sample means are all above the centre line. This is a run of 8, which signals a likely upward shift in the process mean. Investigation shows that a new batch of raw material (Material) was introduced 8 samples ago, slightly increasing the average diameter.

Key Take-aways

  • Quality means conformance to specifications; SQC is the scientific study of variation.
  • The 4 M's (Man, Machine, Material, Method) are the input factors that determine output quality.
  • Chance causes = inherent, small, many — process is in control.
  • Assignable causes = identifiable, large, few — process is out of control.
  • Process control uses control charts (prevention); product control uses acceptance sampling (detection).
  • Shewhart's 3\(\sigma\) control limits give a false-alarm rate of about 0.27%.
  • A control chart flags an out-of-control condition via: points beyond limits, runs, trends, or cycles.
